On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous
By Ocean Vuong

Vuong is one of my favorite poets and novelists. This story is in the perspective of Little Dog, who grapplies with his cultural and sexual identity as well as the power of language, through a series of letters to his illiterate mother.



This is How You Lose the Time War
By Amal El-Mohtar & Max Gladstone

Poetic prose, dynamic storytelling, and a dystopian sci-fi world merge in this novel, where the two protagonists from warring governments exchange letters across different timelines and worlds. Other than the main plot-line, topics in history, philosophy, science, politics, and much more are all discussed.



Babel
By R.F. Kuang

A rising star in literary fiction, R.F. Kuang responds to the beauty and cruelty of traditional academia in this astoundingly complex yet readable novel. Set in an alternate version of 19th-century Oxford, Kuang uses the craft of translations and linguistics to shape a broader perspective on globalization, colonialism, resistance, and moral ambiguity.



Freakonomics
By Steven D. Levitt & Stephen J. Dubner

A surprisingly readable yet informative book on puzzling phenomena and the economic theories behind them. I first read this in middle school and became intrigued by economics as a result. It has grown on me ever since; every time you reread Freakonomics, it offers a deeper insight on something you think you already know.



Crush
By Richard Siken

Haunting, lyrical, and encapsulating the best of contemporary poetry, Richard Siken's work impresses in both literary and emotional depth. Crush is a deeply intimate collection, but also touch on more general topics of fragility, masculinity, and mortality -- all through lines that kept me hanging on every word.



Exhalation
By Ted Chiang

This collection of short stories is varied in length and topic, surprisingly rigorous in its scientific expositions yet a joy to read. From alternate ancient civilizations to a future with literal telegraphic memory, Chiang is masterful at creating absurd technologies and dystopias, then tying them back to fundamental questions and dilemmas in our real world.
